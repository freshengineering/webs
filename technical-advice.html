Specialist Traffic Engineering Advice

Traffic engineering technical advice is specialist counsel for the moments when a project hits a problem that the standard drawings and templates do not solve. On Victorian infrastructure and construction projects, that usually means road safety barrier selection and deflection, traffic staging on live carriageways, or constructability conflicts where the design intent and the realities of site cannot easily coexist. Fresh Engineering provides this advice as an independent, hands-on engineering service rather than a generic report.

These questions carry real consequence. A barrier with insufficient working width can leave a worker or a hazard inside the deflection envelope; a staging plan that ignores sight distance or merge geometry can create a crash risk or stall a project at the DTP review gate. Getting the engineering right early is far cheaper than reworking it during construction, and on major projects a single ill-considered stage can cost weeks of occupancy and significant rework.

With 15+ years across schemes from small council works to North East Link, the West Gate Tunnel, Metro Tunnel, the Level Crossing Removal Project and Barwon Heads Road, we bring the judgement that only comes from having delivered this work in the field. We advise designers, contractors, councils and developers on what will actually be accepted, built and signed off in Victoria.

How We Approach a Technical Problem

Every engagement starts by defining the actual problem, not the symptom. We review the relevant drawings, design speed, traffic data, geometry and site constraints, then interrogate the assumptions behind them. From there we develop and compare options against the governing standards, document the engineering rationale, and recommend a defensible position you can take to the road authority, the client or the design team.

The work is collaborative and iterative. We are comfortable sitting in a design workshop, marking up a staging plan with a contractor, or running barrier deflection checks to confirm working width and offset. The deliverable is a decision you can stand behind, supported by the reasoning that backs it.

  • Problem definition and review of design intent, geometry and traffic data
  • Options development and comparative assessment against governing standards
  • Barrier containment level, working width and deflection checks
  • Staging, constructability and sequencing review against site constraints
  • Clear, documented recommendation with engineering justification

Standards and Authorities

Advice is only useful if it survives scrutiny from the relevant authority. We work to the current Austroads Guide to Road Design and Guide to Traffic Management, the Austroads Guide to Temporary Traffic Management (AGTTM) as adopted by DTP in Victoria, and the relevant DTP/VicRoads supplements, standard drawings and technical notes. Barrier advice is grounded in the applicable containment level, working width and deflection requirements for the system in question, consistent with MASH test levels, and accounts for the design considerations behind both proprietary and standard road safety barrier systems.

Where the work touches the planning system, we account for the relevant planning scheme provisions, such as the car parking requirements under Clause 52.06. We understand what DTP, council and project assurance reviewers look for, which lets us steer a recommendation toward something that will be accepted rather than reworked.

  • Austroads Guide to Road Design and Guide to Traffic Management
  • Austroads Guide to Temporary Traffic Management (AGTTM) as adopted in Victoria
  • DTP/VicRoads supplements, standard drawings and technical notes
  • Barrier containment level, working width and deflection criteria (MASH)
  • Relevant planning scheme provisions (e.g. Clause 52.06) and project assurance requirements

Frequently Asked Questions

When do I need specialist traffic engineering advice rather than a standard design?

You need it when a problem falls outside the standard drawings, such as a barrier that cannot achieve its required deflection within the available offset, a staging plan on a live high-speed road, or a site constraint that puts standards in conflict. It is also worth engaging when a DTP or council reviewer has raised a concern you need to resolve with a defensible engineering position. In short, when the answer is not a template, you need judgement.

How is road safety barrier deflection assessed and why does it matter?

Deflection is the lateral distance a barrier moves when struck, and the system's working width must clear any hazard or worker behind it for the applicable containment level. We check the proposed system's working width and offset against the design speed, vehicle type and site geometry to confirm the hazard sits outside the deflection envelope. Get this wrong and the barrier can fail to protect what it is installed to shield, so it is one of the first things a reviewer will check.

How long does technical advice take to turn around?

A focused question, such as confirming a barrier offset or reviewing a single staging concept, can often be turned around in a few days. A complex constructability problem with multiple options and calculations typically takes one to two weeks. We will give you a realistic timeframe once we understand the problem and the supporting information available, and we can prioritise where a project program demands it.

What drives the cost of a technical advice engagement?

Cost is driven by the complexity of the problem, the number of options to assess, the depth of calculation required (for example detailed barrier deflection or sight distance work), and the level of documentation you need for review or assurance. A short memo confirming a position is a modest fixed fee, while an in-depth options study on a major project is scoped accordingly. We provide a clear quote up front so there are no surprises.
