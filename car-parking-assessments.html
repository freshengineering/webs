Car Parking Demand Assessments

A car parking demand assessment establishes how much parking a development genuinely needs, measures that demand against what the planning scheme requires, and provides the technical justification a responsible authority needs to support the proposal. In Victoria, car parking for most uses is governed by Clause 52.06 of the relevant planning scheme, which sets statutory parking rates by land use under the table at Clause 52.06-5. Where a proposal cannot or should not meet those rates on site, a well-reasoned assessment is what underpins a reduction in the parking requirement under Clause 52.06-3.

The parking question is rarely just about counting bays. Council and the Department of Transport and Planning (DTP, formerly VicRoads) want confidence that a reduced provision will not push overflow onto surrounding streets, compromise safety or erode amenity. A properly evidenced assessment shifts the discussion away from a blunt statutory rate and towards the actual operating demand of the use, supported by empirical parking surveys, accepted demand research and the decision guidelines the responsible authority must weigh.

Our assessment process

We start by confirming the statutory position: the land uses proposed, the applicable Clause 52.06 rates (along with any parking overlay or precinct-specific rate that applies in the relevant municipality), and the resulting statutory car parking requirement. We then build an evidence-based picture of likely demand using empirical parking surveys of comparable sites, published demand data, the operating characteristics of the use, and the site's access to public transport, walking and cycling.

Where a reduction is sought, we quantify the difference between provision and the statutory requirement and assemble the justification against the decision guidelines at Clause 52.06-3. We also test the proposed layout against the design standards so the parking shown actually functions on site.

  • Confirm land uses, Clause 52.06 statutory rates and any overlay or precinct controls
  • Empirical parking surveys and published demand data for comparable uses
  • Assess public transport, walking and cycling access and shared-parking opportunities
  • Quantify provision against statutory requirement and define any reduction sought
  • Justify the reduction against the Clause 52.06-3 decision guidelines
  • Review bay, aisle, ramp gradient and access geometry for compliance and function

Standards and authorities

Assessments are framed around Clause 52.06 of the Victoria Planning Provisions and the relevant municipal planning scheme, including the design standards at Clause 52.06-9 for bay dimensions, gradients, aisle widths and accessway design. We apply AS 2890.1 (off-street car parking), AS 2890.6 (off-street parking for people with disabilities) and AS 2890.2 (off-street commercial vehicle facilities) for layout, and draw on the relevant Austroads guides where access and intersection treatments to the road network are involved.

The responsible authority is generally the local council. DTP is a referral or coordinating road authority where access is proposed to a declared road (an arterial or freeway) or where the use generates significant traffic. We structure each report to address the matters each of these authorities must consider.

  • Clause 52.06 statutory rates and Clause 52.06-3 decision guidelines
  • Clause 52.06-9 design standards for parking layout, gradients and access
  • AS 2890.1, AS 2890.2 and AS 2890.6 parking design standards
  • Austroads Guide to Traffic Management and Guide to Road Design for access
  • Council and DTP referral and approval requirements

Who it's for

Car parking assessments are most often needed by developers, architects, town planners and landowners pursuing a planning permit, particularly where the proposal seeks fewer spaces than Clause 52.06 requires, introduces a new or intensified use, or sits in an established area sensitive to on-street parking pressure.

Frequently Asked Questions

When is a car parking demand assessment required for a planning permit in Victoria?

It is typically required when a development cannot meet the parking rates set by Clause 52.06 and a reduction in the requirement is sought, or when council requests one to support a new, intensified or change-of-use application. It is also commonly needed where the site is in an established area sensitive to on-street parking, or where a use such as childcare, medical or hospitality has variable demand that the statutory rate captures poorly. The assessment provides the evidence the responsible authority needs to grant a permit with a reduced provision.

How does a car parking reduction under Clause 52.06 work?

Clause 52.06-3 allows the responsible authority to reduce, including to zero, the number of car spaces the planning scheme requires, provided the reduction is justified. Justification is tested against the decision guidelines, which consider the likely demand of the use, access to public transport and active transport, existing on-street parking conditions, and any adopted parking precinct plan. A well-prepared assessment quantifies the real demand and demonstrates that the reduced provision will operate without unreasonable impact on the surrounding network.

How long does a car parking assessment take to prepare?

A straightforward assessment is generally turned around in one to two weeks once plans and the brief are confirmed. Timeframes extend where empirical parking surveys of comparable sites are required, since surveys must be carried out on representative days and need to avoid school holidays, public holidays and other atypical periods. We confirm a timeline for your specific project at the outset.

What factors affect the cost of a car parking assessment?

Cost is driven by the scale and complexity of the use, whether a reduction is being sought, and the amount of supporting evidence required. A single-use application relying on established demand data costs less than a mixed-use proposal needing multiple empirical parking surveys and a detailed layout review. Matters proceeding to VCAT, which require expert evidence and attendance, attract additional fees.
