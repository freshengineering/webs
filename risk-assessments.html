Safety Risk Assessments

A safety risk assessment is a formal, documented process for identifying hazards on a road or transport network, or within a worksite, and putting controls in place to reduce the likelihood and severity of harm. Rather than relying on individual judgement, it applies a structured methodology - hazard identification, risk analysis, evaluation against agreed criteria, and treatment - so decisions are defensible, consistent and traceable. For traffic and road safety work in Victoria, this is the mechanism that turns 'we think this is unsafe' into a ranked, evidence-based set of actions.

In Victorian practice, risk assessment underpins almost everything from temporary works through to permanent network changes. The Austroads Guide to Temporary Traffic Management (AGTTM), which forms the basis of current DTP worksite practice, embeds risk-based thinking at every stage of planning and approving traffic management. Austroads' broader safe system guidance and network safety planning apply the same logic to permanent infrastructure. A robust assessment is also central to discharging duties under the Occupational Health and Safety Act 2004 (Vic) and OHS Regulations 2017, including the duties owed by designers, employers and principal contractors, alongside road authority obligations.

Our risk assessment process

We follow the established risk management cycle set out in AS ISO 31000 and reflected throughout the AGTTM and Austroads safe system guides. The work begins by establishing the context and scope - the section of network, worksite or activity being assessed, the road users and workers exposed, and the criteria against which risk will be judged. From there we systematically identify hazards, analyse each in terms of likelihood and consequence, evaluate the resulting risk rating, and recommend controls applied in order of the hierarchy of control: eliminate, then substitute, isolate, engineer, administer, and only lastly rely on PPE.

Crucially, the process does not stop at the rating. We document residual risk after controls are applied so the client can make an informed decision on whether that level is acceptable, and we set out monitoring and review triggers so the assessment stays current as conditions change.

  • Establish context, scope and the road users / workers exposed
  • Identify hazards through site inspection, plan review and crash history where relevant
  • Analyse likelihood and consequence using a defined risk matrix
  • Evaluate and rank risks against agreed acceptance criteria
  • Recommend controls using the hierarchy of control
  • Document residual risk and set review / monitoring triggers

Standards, authorities and methodology

The framework we apply is anchored in the documents Victorian authorities actually rely on. The Austroads Guide to Temporary Traffic Management governs worksite and temporary works risk; the Austroads Guide to Road Safety and DTP's network safety processes inform permanent treatments; and AS ISO 31000 provides the underlying risk management discipline. Where the work touches DTP-managed (declared) roads, we align with DTP requirements and approval expectations, and for parking, access or development matters we reference the relevant Victoria Planning Provisions, such as Clause 52.06.

Our assessments are prepared by personnel with the credentials these authorities expect - Traffic Management Designer (TMD) accreditation across Categories 1, 2 and 3, and accredited Road Safety Auditor status - so the methodology and the people behind it both withstand review.

  • Austroads Guide to Temporary Traffic Management (AGTTM)
  • Austroads Guide to Road Safety and safe system principles
  • AS ISO 31000 risk management framework
  • DTP / VicRoads requirements and approval expectations
  • OHS Act 2004 (Vic) designer, employer and principal contractor duties
  • Relevant planning provisions (e.g. Clause 52.06)

Frequently Asked Questions

When is a safety risk assessment required for traffic works in Victoria?

A risk assessment is expected whenever works are high-risk, non-standard, or depart from the typical layouts in the AGTTM and DTP guidance - for example complex worksites, unusual staging, or treatments without a standard solution. It is also commonly required as a condition of a DTP or council approval, or to discharge OHS designer, employer and principal contractor duties. In practice, if a hazard cannot be managed by simply applying a standard arrangement, a documented assessment is the appropriate step.

How long does a safety risk assessment take to prepare?

A focused worksite or single-location assessment can typically be turned around within a few business days of receiving the site information and plans. More complex network or multi-stage assessments, or those requiring a site inspection and crash-history review, generally take one to two weeks. We confirm a timeframe once the scope and inputs are clear.

What factors affect the cost of a risk assessment?

Cost is driven mainly by the size and complexity of the site or network, the number of hazards and stages involved, whether a physical site inspection is needed, and the depth of analysis required by the approving authority. Assessments that must integrate with traffic management plans or design packages, or address the heightened scrutiny on major projects, involve more effort. We scope and quote each job individually rather than apply a flat rate.

Who is qualified to carry out a road safety risk assessment?

For traffic and worksite risk, the work should be done by personnel with relevant accreditation - typically a Traffic Management Designer (TMD) and, for permanent network safety matters, an accredited Road Safety Auditor. These credentials demonstrate the assessor understands AGTTM, Austroads and DTP requirements and applies a defensible methodology.
