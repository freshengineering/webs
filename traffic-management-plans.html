Traffic Management Plans & CTIA in Victoria

A Traffic Management Plan (TMP) is the controlling document for how road users, pedestrians, cyclists and workers are kept safe when works occupy or affect a road reserve. In Victoria, temporary traffic management is designed in accordance with the Austroads Guide to Temporary Traffic Management (AGTTM), which the Department of Transport and Planning (DTP) has adopted, with a Victorian supplement, in place of the former VicRoads worksite traffic management requirements. Whether you are resurfacing a residential street for a council, constructing a subdivision frontage, or staging works on a declared arterial under DTP jurisdiction, a compliant TMP is the difference between a permit being issued and a worksite being shut down.

A Construction Traffic Impact Assessment (CTIA) sits alongside the TMP and addresses the broader picture: how construction traffic, heavy-vehicle movements, site access and laydown will interact with the surrounding network over the life of a project. Councils and DTP commonly require a CTIA as a planning permit condition for medium and large developments, particularly where construction truck routes pass schools, activity centres or quiet residential streets.

Standards and authorities we work to

Temporary traffic management in Victoria is governed by AGTTM and its Victorian supplement, supported by the relevant Austroads guides and Australian Standards. The approving authority depends on who manages the road: DTP (formerly VicRoads) for declared arterials and freeways, and the local council for municipal roads. Knowing which authority and instrument applies, and getting the application right the first time, is what keeps a project on program.

  • Austroads Guide to Temporary Traffic Management (AGTTM) and the DTP Victorian supplement
  • DTP / VicRoads requirements and MoA process for works on declared and arterial roads
  • Local council road occupation and consent to work within road reserve permits
  • AS 1742.3 traffic control for works on roads, where it continues to apply
  • Memorandum of Authorisation (MoA) preparation and lodgement on declared roads

Frequently Asked Questions

When do I need a Traffic Management Plan in Victoria?

You need a TMP whenever works will occupy, obstruct or affect part of a road, footpath or nature strip, including lane closures, parking removal, crossover construction or works that change how road users move through an area. On council-managed roads it is required to obtain a road occupation or consent to work within road reserve permit, and on DTP-declared roads a Memorandum of Authorisation (MoA) is required. In practice, almost any works inside the road reserve will trigger the requirement.

What is the difference between a TMP and a Construction Traffic Impact Assessment?

A TMP is the on-site traffic management design, covering signs, devices, lane arrangements and worker safety for the works themselves, prepared to AGTTM by an accredited Traffic Management Designer. A CTIA is a higher-level report assessing how construction and heavy-vehicle traffic will affect the surrounding network over the life of a project, including truck routes, access points and impacts on nearby uses. Larger developments often need both: the CTIA to satisfy a planning permit condition and the TMP to obtain the works permit.

How long does it take to get a TMP approved?

The design itself can usually be turned around within a few business days once we have the site details and scope. Approval timeframes then depend on the authority: council permits and DTP MoAs commonly take from one to several weeks, and high-impact works on arterial roads can require additional lead time for review and coordination. We recommend engaging us early so the application is lodged well ahead of your planned start date.

What affects the cost of a TMP or CTIA?

Cost is driven by the complexity of the road environment (speed, traffic volumes and the TMD category required), the number of stages, the duration of the works and the level of authority review involved. A short-term, low-speed residential job is far simpler than staged works on a high-volume arterial. A CTIA for a development is scoped separately and depends on project size and the planning conditions it must address; we provide a fixed fee once we understand your project.
