Independent Road Safety Audits

A road safety audit (RSA) is a formal, independent assessment of a road project or existing road by qualified auditors, carried out to identify the crash risk to all road users before problems become incidents. Rather than checking whether a design complies with the relevant drawings and standards, an audit asks a sharper question: how could this road, intersection or worksite contribute to someone being killed or seriously injured, and what can be done to reduce that risk? Fresh Engineering provides accredited road safety audits across the Austroads project design stages, together with audits of existing roads and road works, throughout metropolitan Melbourne and regional Victoria.

In Victoria, road safety auditing sits at the heart of the Safe System approach adopted by the Department of Transport and Planning (DTP), local councils and infrastructure delivery agencies. Audits are routinely required on DTP works, are commonly conditioned through the planning permit process, and are expected on major projects under the Level Crossing Removal Program, the Victorian Big Build and similar programs. An audit carried out early, at the feasibility or concept design stage, is far cheaper to act on than a defect identified after a road opens to traffic.

As an Accredited Road Safety Auditor with Traffic Management Designer (TMD) Cat 1/2/3 credentials and more than 15 years on projects ranging from single council intersections to North East Link, the West Gate Tunnel, the Metro Tunnel and Barwon Heads Road, we bring genuine design and traffic management understanding to every audit. The result is a report that does not just list problems, but explains the risk, ranks it and offers practical, buildable recommendations.

Audit stages and the audit process

Road safety audits follow the methodology in the Austroads Guide to Road Safety Part 6, which defines distinct stages so that risks are caught at the point where they are cheapest to fix. We undertake audits at any stage of a project's life, matching the depth of review to the maturity of the design and the information available, and we also audit existing roads and traffic management at active road works.

Every audit runs the same disciplined process: a briefing and acceptance of the audit brief, a structured review of the supplied drawings and reports, a site inspection (including a night inspection where relevant), identification and risk-rating of findings against the Safe System, and a written report. The client or design team then formally responds to each finding, closing out the risks raised.

  • Stage 1 - Feasibility: route options, layout concepts and the basic safety of the scheme before commitment
  • Stage 2 - Concept (preliminary) design: geometry, sight distance, intersection form and provision for pedestrians, cyclists and other vulnerable road users
  • Stage 3 - Detailed design: signs, line marking, safety barriers, lighting, drainage and constructability of the final drawings
  • Stage 4 - Pre-opening: a pre-opening site inspection, including a night inspection, before the road is opened to traffic
  • Existing roads and road works: in-service audits of operating roads and known crash locations, plus audits of temporary traffic management at active worksites

Standards and authorities

Our audits are conducted against the relevant Victorian and national references so the findings carry weight with the approving authority. The governing document is the Austroads Guide to Road Safety Part 6 (Road Safety Audit), supported by the Austroads Guide to Road Design and Guide to Traffic Management, the relevant DTP supplements and standard drawings, and the Australian Standards for signs and markings (the AS 1742 series).

Where temporary traffic management at a worksite is being audited, findings are assessed against the Austroads Guide to Temporary Traffic Management (AGTTM) as adopted in Victoria. Audits are independent by definition: the auditor is separate from the design team, which is exactly what DTP, councils and Tier 1 contractors expect to see on the public record.

  • Austroads Guide to Road Safety Part 6 (Road Safety Audit) - the core methodology
  • Austroads Guide to Road Design and Guide to Traffic Management
  • DTP supplements, standard drawings and Safe System policy
  • AS 1742 series (traffic control devices) and AGTTM for temporary traffic management
  • Auditor independent of, and separate from, the project's design team

What you receive

The deliverable is a clear, defensible audit report you can hand to a client, council or DTP with confidence. Each finding is described with its location, the road users at risk, a Safe System risk rating combining the likelihood and severity of a crash, and a specific, practical recommendation. We avoid vague observations and box-ticking; the aim is findings a designer can actually action.

  • A formal audit report listing each finding by location and risk rating
  • Risk ratings applied consistently using the Austroads frequency and severity matrix
  • Practical, buildable recommendations rather than generic advice
  • Annotated plans and site photographs, including night inspection imagery where relevant
  • A response and sign-off table so the design team can formally close out each finding
  • An optional briefing or close-out meeting to walk through the findings

Frequently Asked Questions

When is a road safety audit required in Victoria?

Audits are routinely required on DTP projects and are frequently imposed as a planning permit condition for traffic-generating developments and new access points. They are also expected on major infrastructure packages under programs such as the Level Crossing Removal Program and the Victorian Big Build, and are commonly carried out voluntarily before a council or developer commits to a design. If you are unsure whether your project triggers an audit, check the permit conditions or project brief early, because a Stage 2 audit is far cheaper to act on than a Stage 4 finding.

How long does a road safety audit take?

A straightforward single-intersection or small subdivision audit can typically be turned around within one to two weeks of receiving the drawings, allowing time for the site inspection and a night visit where needed. Larger or multi-stage projects take longer and are usually programmed around the design milestones. We can often expedite where a deadline is tight, but a rushed audit on incomplete information serves no one.

What affects the cost of a road safety audit?

Cost is driven mainly by the scale and complexity of the scheme, the audit stage, the size of the audit team required, and whether a night inspection or multiple site visits are needed. A small council intersection audit is modest; a staged audit on a major arterial or a Big Build package is a larger commitment. We provide a fixed fee once we understand the scope and the drawings available.

What is the difference between a road safety audit and a design compliance check?

A compliance check confirms that a design meets the relevant standards and drawings; a road safety audit asks how the road could contribute to a crash, and to death or serious injury, even where the design is technically compliant. The two are complementary, but only an independent audit by an accredited auditor delivers the risk-based, Safe System assessment that DTP and councils expect on the record.
